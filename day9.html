What does each cut change? What stays the same? Understanding visual rhythm on paper is the same skill as assembling clips in the sequence editor.

Advanced Compositing

Animation

Day 6 introduced the compositor — today we go deeper. Render Passes split your scene into separate image layers (diffuse color, shadows, specular highlights, reflections, depth) that can be adjusted and recombined with full control after the render. Grade shadows and highlights independently. Composite characters against new backgrounds using alpha channels and the Alpha Over node. Use the Z-depth pass with a Defocus node to add cinematic depth of field entirely in post — no re-rendering needed.

Enabling Render Passes
01
Enable passes in View Layer Properties
In View Layer Properties (the camera-with-layers icon), open the Passes section. Enable Diffuse Color, Shadow, and Z-Depth at minimum. Each enabled pass becomes a separate output socket on the Render Layers node in the compositor.
View Layer Properties → Passes → enable as needed
02
Access passes in the compositor
Open the Compositor workspace and check Use Nodes. The Render Layers node now shows a socket for each enabled pass — drag a link from any pass socket to inspect it with a Viewer node, or pipe it into a separate processing chain.
Ctrl Shift Left Click on any node to preview
03
Grade shadows independently
Route the Shadow pass through an RGB Curves node. Darken it, tint it, or soften its edges, then combine it back with the image using a Mix node in Multiply mode. You can adjust shadow darkness and color without touching the rest of the scene.
Shift+A → Color → RGB Curves  ·  Shift+A → Color → Mix
04
Add depth of field with the Z pass
Route the Z (depth) pass into a Defocus node's Z socket, and the combined image into its Image socket. Set the Bokeh type and F-stop to control how strongly background elements blur. Tweak focus distance without re-rendering a single frame.
Shift+A → Filter → Defocus
05
Composite elements with Alpha Over
To place a 3D render over a background image, connect your render to the upper Image socket of an Alpha Over node and the background to the lower. The alpha channel of your render acts as the mask. Stack multiple Alpha Over nodes to layer multiple elements.
Shift+A → Color → Alpha Over

Video Editing & Compositing

Animation

Blender's Video Sequence Editor (VSE) is a full non-linear video editor built right into the same application as your 3D work. Import multiple video clips onto separate tracks, cut and trim footage, and layer your 3D renders directly on top of real video. Add transitions, color grades, and text overlays to pull everything into a cohesive final piece — no external editor required. This is how your 3D animations and composited renders become part of a complete, watchable video.

Video Sequence Editor Shortcuts
Editing Controls
Shift A
Add a clip — Movie, Image, Scene, Sound, or Color strip
K
Cut (soft cut) at the playhead — splits a strip in two
Shift K
Hard cut — splits and removes the handles
G
Grab / move a selected strip on the timeline
Space
Play / pause preview
Multi-Clip Video Workflow
01
Open the Video Editing workspace
Click the Video Editing tab at the top of the screen. The layout splits into a preview on top and the sequence timeline below. Set your output resolution and frame rate in Output Properties before importing anything — everything will conform to these settings.
Workspace tabs → Video Editing
02
Import your video clips
In the sequencer, press Shift+A and choose Movie to browse for a video file. Each clip lands as a strip on channel 1. Import additional clips the same way — Blender stacks them on higher channels automatically, or you can drag them manually to any channel.
Shift A → Movie
03
Layer clips on separate channels
Channels stack from bottom to top — higher channel numbers appear on top in the final output. Put your background footage on channel 1, overlay clips on channel 2, and titles or effects on channel 3 and above. Clips on higher channels always render over lower ones.
04
Cut, trim, and arrange
Move the playhead to where you want a cut and press K to split a strip. Drag the left or right edge of any strip to trim it. Grab strips with G to slide them along the timeline. Delete unwanted sections with X. Build your edit by arranging the trimmed pieces in sequence.
K cut  ·  G move  ·  X delete
05
Add a 3D render as a layer
Press Shift+A and choose Scene to add your current Blender scene as a strip. It renders live in the sequence — your 3D animation composited directly over video footage below it. Use the Blend drop-down on the strip to control how it combines with the layer underneath (Alpha Over to use transparency).
Shift A → Scene  ·  Strip Properties → Blend: Alpha Over
06
Export to video
In Output Properties, set File Format to FFmpeg Video, Container to MPEG-4, and Video Codec to H.264. This gives you a standard .mp4 that plays everywhere. Set a destination folder and press Ctrl+F12 to render the full edit to a single video file.
Ctrl F12 Render animation → outputs .mp4

Mixing & Editing

Sound Design

Bring all your recorded and designed audio together. Balance levels so nothing is too loud or buried, shape frequencies with EQ, add reverb and spatial width to give sounds depth and placement. Build a final audio timeline that supports your animation — tight enough to feel intentional, spacious enough to breathe.
