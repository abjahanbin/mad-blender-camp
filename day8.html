Shaders & Materials

Modeling / 3D

Shaders define how a surface interacts with light — the difference between polished metal and rough concrete, frosted glass and clear water. Blender's Shader Editor builds materials as node networks: inputs, operations, and outputs wired together to describe any surface you can imagine. The Principled BSDF puts the most important controls in one node — Base Color, Roughness (matte to glossy), Metallic, and Emission (self-glow). Connect an image texture for surface detail, use a Color Ramp to map values, or paint directly on the mesh with texture paint.

Key Principled BSDF Inputs
01
Base Color
The base tint of the surface before lighting is applied. Connect an Image Texture node here to apply a photo or painted texture, or just click the color swatch to pick a flat color.
02
Roughness
Controls how blurry or sharp reflections are. 0 = mirror-perfect. 1 = completely diffuse. Most real surfaces live between 0.2 (polished wood) and 0.8 (rough concrete). This single value has the biggest impact on the feel of a material.
03
Metallic
0 = non-metal (plastic, fabric, skin). 1 = metal (reflects its surroundings with a tinted color). In practice it's almost always exactly 0 or exactly 1 — metals and non-metals behave fundamentally differently under light.
04
Emission
Makes the surface emit its own light — useful for screens, neon, fire, or glowing effects. Connect a color and raise the strength. In Cycles, emission surfaces actually illuminate the objects around them.

Lighting in Blender

Modeling / 3D

Lighting transforms a grey scene into something that feels real, atmospheric, and alive. We'll work through Blender's four light types — Point (a bare omnidirectional bulb), Sun (parallel rays, like daylight), Spot (a focused cone), and Area (a soft rectangular panel) — and explore HDRI environment maps for photographic background illumination. Then we'll set up the classic three-point lighting rig used in photography and film: a key light for definition, a fill light to soften harsh shadows, and a rim light to separate your subject from the background.

Three-Point Lighting Setup
01
Place the key light
Add an Area light and position it 45° to the side and slightly above your subject. This is your primary light — it defines the shape and casts the main shadows. Turn up the power until the lit side of your subject looks well-exposed.
02
Add a fill light
Add a second, dimmer light on the opposite side of the key. The fill lightens the shadow side so it isn't completely black — set its power to roughly a quarter of the key light. Keep it soft and low to avoid creating a second competing shadow.
03
Add a rim light
Position a third light behind and above the subject, aimed at the back of the head and shoulders. This creates a bright edge (a "rim") that separates the subject from the background and gives depth to the silhouette.
04
Adjust color temperature
Give each light a slightly different color to create contrast. Key light slightly warm (orange tint), fill light slightly cool (blue tint). The difference between warm and cool in the same scene reads as natural and photographic.
Object Data → Color — shift the hue slightly, keep it subtle

Ambience, Music & Emotional Beats

Sound Design

Sound design is emotional design. Learn how ambient layers, musical key and mode, tempo, and dynamic range shape the feeling of a scene. A room that's silent is tense. The same room with a low drone is ominous. With gentle strings it's melancholic. We'll compose a short atmospheric piece that gives a 3D world its emotional texture.
