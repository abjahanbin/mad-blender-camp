Advanced and Alternative Modeling Techniques

Modeling / 3D

Not everything needs to be built from polygons. Metaballs grow and merge like blobs of liquid — perfect for organic shapes, creatures, and fluid forms. The Skin Modifier lets you sketch a wireframe skeleton of vertices and inflate it instantly into a character mesh. We'll explore these procedural and non-destructive tools that generate complex forms in ways traditional box modeling can't.

3D Sculpting in Blender

Modeling / 3D

Move beyond box modeling into organic form. Blender's sculpt mode lets you push, pull, pinch, and smooth geometry like digital clay. No more boxy topology — sculpt faces, creatures, terrain, or whatever shape is in your head.

Sculpt Setup — Step by Step
01
Block out the form
Use primitive objects to roughly build the overall shape. A character might be a sphere for the head, a cylinder for the torso, capsules for limbs. Proportions only — no detail yet.
Shift A Add mesh
02
Apply scale to every object
Select each piece and apply its scale before sculpting. An unapplied scale makes brushes behave inconsistently — strokes in one direction feel stronger than another.
Ctrl A → Scale
03
Join into one mesh
Select all your blocking pieces and join them into a single object. Sculpt mode works on one mesh at a time — separate objects stay separate.
A select all, then Ctrl J
04
Voxel Remesh
Your blocking geometry is jagged and uneven. Remesh replaces it with a uniform grid of polygons that brushes can push cleanly. Smaller voxel size = more detail. Remesh again any time topology gets messy.
Ctrl R in Sculpt Mode
05
Sculpt — large forms first
Enter Sculpt Mode and work with a large brush on big proportions. Progressively shrink the brush as you move into surface detail. Add the story of the shape before you add the texture of it.
Ctrl Tab → Sculpt Mode
